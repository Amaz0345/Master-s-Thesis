Theoretical Framework

Cash Transfers & Intimate Partner Violence

Two competing theoretical pathways linking improved household economic conditions to changes in IPV incidence

Intervention

Cash Transfer Programme

Direct financial transfer to women in the household

Women's Bargaining Power Increases

Improved economic conditions strengthen women's negotiating position within the household dynamic

A

Acceptance & Adaptation

Male Acceptance Mechanism
Women assert greater agency and decision-making power within the home as economic dependence on male partner decreases
Men adapt to the shift in household dynamics, accepting the rebalancing of power without perceiving it as a threat to status
Household cooperation improves; reduced financial stress diminishes conflict triggers
↘
IPV Decreases
Economic empowerment translates to reduced violence
B

Resistance & Reprisal

Male Backlash Mechanism
Women attempt to assert agency and renegotiate power within the household, challenging existing gender norms
Men perceive the shift as a threat to masculine identity and household authority, responding with resistance and coercive control
Violence is used instrumentally to reassert dominance and punish women for challenging the power hierarchy
↗
IPV Increases
Backlash effect — women face reprisals for empowerment

The direction of the relationship between cash transfers and IPV remains empirically contested. Outcomes may vary by programme design, cultural context, targeting, and whether complementary gender-norm interventions are included.
